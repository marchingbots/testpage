Starting a Business in Indonesia

Success starts with compliance.

Foreign founders come to Indonesia seeking opportunity.
Durable ventures begin with compliant foundations.

Do not invest first and ask compliance questions later.

In Indonesia, early setup errors can affect licensing, land use, immigration, operations, and long-term commercial flexibility. The cost of correcting a non-compliant structure is often far greater than the cost of establishing the business properly from the outset.

Why This Matters

The first decisions shape the whole project.

Indonesia’s framework is manageable when approached in the right sequence. The problem is not usually the law itself. The problem is entering the market in the wrong order.

Wrong activity, wrong structure.

If the business activity is not properly defined at the outset, the company can end up with a structure, licensing pathway, or commercial model that does not align with what it actually intends to do.

Money gets trapped in the setup.

Once a lease is signed, capital is deployed, or contractors are engaged, founders lose flexibility. At that point, fixing compliance issues becomes a recovery exercise rather than a clean launch.

Cleanup is harder than prevention.

It is almost always easier to design the setup correctly than to unwind a location, restructure an activity, or repair a licensing position after the business is already underway.

Core Concepts

The basic framework foreign investors need to understand.

You do not need to master the legal terminology, but you do need to understand the moving parts that determine whether a business can be launched properly.

Business actor

The party carrying out the business

In the Indonesian framework, a business actor is the individual or entity conducting business activities in a particular field.

KBLI

The activity classification

The KBLI code helps define what the business is actually doing. This influences risk level, licensing expectations, and the broader setup strategy.

NIB

The core registration identity

The NIB is the business identification number used within Indonesia’s licensing framework and is a key milestone in the establishment process.

Foundational approvals

Location, environment, buildings

Depending on the project, the business may need to address spatial conformity, environmental matters, and building-related compliance before operating.

The Sequence

How a compliant business setup should be approached.

Indonesian regulation distinguishes between starting a business and running a business. For founders, the practical lesson is simple: the market-entry sequence matters. Each step supports the next.

01
Starting a business

Define the intended business activity properly

Before any capital is committed, the intended activity should be clearly defined. This is where the business model, service offering, and commercial reality must be aligned with the appropriate regulatory classification.

02
Starting a business

Establish the correct legal vehicle

The company or business structure should be suitable for the activity, the investor profile, and the way the business will actually operate. A rushed or ill-fitting structure can create downstream friction across licensing and operations.

03
Starting a business

Secure core business legality

Once the business actor is properly established, the core registration steps follow, including the business identification framework that supports the licensing process.

04
Starting a business

Test the location and compliance fit

A location should not be treated as purely commercial. The compliance position of the premises, land use, and wider project setting can all affect whether the intended activity can be lawfully and commercially sustained there.

05
Starting a business

Address foundational approvals before launch

Depending on the nature of the business, approvals relating to location, environment, immigration, or building use may need to be understood and staged correctly before operations begin.

06
Running a business

Prepare the business for real operation

Only after the front-end legal and compliance position is sound should the business move into site preparation, contracting, staffing, fit-out, procurement, and operational readiness.

07
Running a business

Meet the standards tied to the activity

Some businesses require more than mere registration. Sector-specific standards, operational conditions, and supporting requirements may all need to be satisfied before commercial activity is carried out in earnest.

08
Running a business

Launch on a compliant footing

The goal is not simply to open. The goal is to open with a business model, location, structure, and compliance position that can actually support stable operations in Indonesia.

Commercial Reality

What this means for founders entering the market.

Do not treat setup as paperwork.

In Indonesia, setup is not a formality sitting alongside the business plan. It is part of the business plan. The company structure, activity classification, premises, and approval pathway all shape what can actually be built.

Do not assume a location is usable just because it is available.

Investors are often drawn to a site before testing whether it suits the intended business activity from a compliance perspective. That is one of the most common ways money gets committed too early.

Do not confuse speed with progress.

Moving quickly without understanding the compliance path can produce the appearance of momentum while increasing legal and commercial risk. A slightly slower start is often what protects the project.

Important note

This page is intentionally strategic rather than exhaustive. It is designed to help clients understand why sequencing matters, not to replace a project-specific setup and licensing review.

Client Questions

Questions founders often ask too late.

Can I secure the premises first and deal with compliance afterwards?

That is often where the trouble begins. Once the business is financially tied to a site, the founder’s negotiating power and strategic flexibility are reduced.

Can I begin operating once the company exists on paper?

Not necessarily. Establishing the entity is only one step. The real question is whether the business can carry out its intended activity from that structure, at that location, with the approvals its model requires.

Is a cleanup later a realistic strategy?

Sometimes issues can be corrected, but remediation is usually more expensive, slower, and more disruptive than designing the setup correctly before capital is committed.

What is the safest starting point?

The safest starting point is to define the activity, test the intended structure and location, and understand the likely compliance path before the project becomes commercially locked in.
